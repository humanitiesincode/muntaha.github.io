Introduction

India’s multilingual landscape has never been flat or evenly distributed. Instead, linguistic and cultural communities have historically existed within overlapping, hierarchical, and regionally specific ecologies, shaped by migration, ecology, state policy, economic extraction, and social marginalisation. Many of these communities—particularly indigenous and minoritised language groups—remain underrepresented in official archives, demographic visualisations, and digital knowledge infrastructures. This project undertakes a comparative cultural-linguistic mapping of three regions in eastern and central India: Jharkhand, West Bengal, and the Dandakaranya region (covering parts of Chhattisgarh, Odisha, Telangana, Andhra Pradesh, and Maharashtra). The aim is to examine how language, culture, geography, and power intersect spatially and temporally, producing uneven patterns of survival, erosion, and displacement.
